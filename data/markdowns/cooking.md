# Cooking Basics

## Chapter 1: Knife Skills

The chef's knife is the most important tool in the kitchen. Hold it with a pinch
grip: thumb and forefinger gripping the blade, remaining fingers around the handle.
Always use a claw grip on the food to protect your fingertips.

Basic cuts:
- Dice: uniform cubes, typically 1/4 inch for small, 1/2 inch for medium
- Julienne: thin matchstick strips, about 1/8 inch square by 2 inches long
- Chiffonade: stacking leafy herbs, rolling, and slicing into thin ribbons

## Chapter 2: The Five Mother Sauces

French cuisine defines five fundamental sauces:
- Béchamel: milk-based, thickened with white roux
- Velouté: light stock-based, thickened with blond roux
- Espagnole: brown stock-based, thickened with brown roux
- Sauce Tomat: tomato-based, often with pork and vegetables
- Hollandaise: emulsified egg yolk and butter, acidified with lemon

## Chapter 3: Heat Management

Understanding heat is essential for cooking:
- Searing requires very high heat to trigger the Maillard reaction
- Sautéing uses medium-high heat with small amounts of fat
- Simmering is gentle heat (185-205°F) with small bubbles breaking the surface
- Poaching is the gentlest wet cooking method (160-180°F)

## Chapter 4: Baking Science

Baking is chemistry you can eat. Key principles:
- Gluten development gives structure; overworking creates toughness
- Leavening agents (yeast, baking powder, baking soda) create rise
- The Maillard reaction and caramelization create browning and flavor
- Temperature control is critical: most cakes bake at 350°F

## Chapter 5: Food Safety

The temperature danger zone is 40°F to 140°F where bacteria multiply rapidly.
Keep hot food hot (above 140°F) and cold food cold (below 40°F).
Cook poultry to 165°F internal temperature, ground meats to 160°F.
